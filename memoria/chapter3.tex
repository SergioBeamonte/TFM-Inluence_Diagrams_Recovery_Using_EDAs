\chapter{Implementation}
\label{ch:implementation}

This chapter describes the proof-of-concept that turns the
parameter-recovery problem of Chapter~\ref{ch:framework} into a running
program: given an Influence Diagram and a handful of expert decision
rules, recover a parameter vector $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ that reproduces
those rules. Rather than walking through the code module by module, we
describe what each part of the pipeline does and why it was designed
that way.

The system builds on three external components:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textsc{SMILE} and its \texttt{pysmile} bindings parse the
        \texttt{.xdsl} model and evaluate the diagram with the Shachter
        algorithm~\cite{shachter1986evaluating,ezawa1994evidence,genie};
  \item \texttt{NumPy} holds the probability and utility tables in
        memory;
  \item \textsc{EDAspy}~\cite{soloviev2024edaspy} provides the EDA used
        as the optimiser.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Around them the pipeline has four stages, sketched in
Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}: (i)~load the diagram, (ii)~read the
expert rules, (iii)~turn a candidate parameter vector into a score by
evaluating the diagram against the rules, and (iv)~let the EDA search
for the vector that maximises that score.

% ==============================================================
\section{From the diagram and the rules to a search problem}
% ==============================================================

The diagram is read once with \texttt{pysmile}, which exposes every
node, its parents and its numerical table. The pipeline keeps a
reference to each table so that a candidate solution can be written back
in place and the diagram re-evaluated without rebuilding the network
every time: the evaluation is by far the most expensive operation, so it
is repeated as little as possible.

The expert rules are read from a CSV file with one rule per row. Each row
records the observed state of the relevant variables and the action the
expert considers optimal for the target decision; a sign convention
distinguishes the observed variables from the target decision and marks
the columns that play no role in that rule. Only rows that actually name
a target decision are kept. The result is a list of
\emph{(evidence, decision, expected action)} triples that the scoring
step will check one by one.

\section{Encoding and decoding the parameters}
% ==============================================================

The EDA searches over plain real vectors, but the parameters of an ID
are constrained: every CPT row is a probability distribution and the
utilities live on a bounded scale. The encoding bridges the two. All the
free entries of the CPTs and of the utility table are concatenated into a
single vector $\boldsymbol{\phi}\in[0,1]^{d}$, which is exactly the kind
of box-bounded space the continuous UMDA samples from; a fixed node
ordering lets the decoder recover which slice of $\boldsymbol{\phi}$
belongs to each table.

Decoding is where the constraints are enforced, so that \emph{every}
vector the EDA produces maps to a legal model and the Shachter engine can
always be called:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Chance nodes.} Each CPT row is obtained by passing the
        corresponding entries of $\boldsymbol{\phi}$ through a softmax,
        which always returns non-negative values that sum to one.
  \item \textbf{Utility node.} Each free utility entry is mapped through
        a sigmoid onto a bounded range (here $[0,10]$), and two entries
        are pinned to the best and worst outcomes declared by the expert.
\end{itemize}

\begin{proposition}[Validity of the decoded model]
\label{prop:valid-cpt}
For every $\boldsymbol{\phi}\in[0,1]^{d}$ the decoded tables form a valid
parameterisation: each CPT row lies on the probability simplex
$\Delta(\Omega_{C_i})$ and every utility lies in the chosen range. Hence
the Shachter engine can be evaluated unconditionally, with no need to
reject or repair samples.
\end{proposition}

The two pinned utility entries are not cosmetic. Because the optimal
policy depends only on the \emph{ordering} of the utilities, $u$ is
identifiable only up to a positive affine transformation; fixing the
best and worst outcomes removes that ambiguity and stops the EDA from
drifting along a direction that does not change the policy. The same
partial-identifiability phenomenon is studied formally by Lazzati and
Metelli~\cite{lazzati2025utilities} for utility learning in MDPs.

\section{Scoring a candidate}
% ==============================================================

A candidate is judged by how many expert rules it reproduces. Since
\textsc{EDAspy} minimises, we use the complementary \emph{cost}:

\begin{definition}[Cost function]
\label{def:cost}
\[
   c(\boldsymbol{\phi}) = 1 -
   \frac{1}{|\mathcal{R}|}
   \sum_{(\mathbf{x}^{(l)}, D_{i_l}, a^{(l)})\in\mathcal{R}}
   \mathbb{1}\!\left[\delta^{*}_{i_l}\!\left(\boldsymbol{\Theta}
   (\boldsymbol{\phi}),\mathbf{x}^{(l)}\right) = a^{(l)}\right],
\]
where $\boldsymbol{\Theta}(\boldsymbol{\phi})$ is the decoded vector and
$\delta^{*}_{i_l}$ is the action the Shachter engine returns under the
evidence $\mathbf{x}^{(l)}$.
\end{definition}

Evaluating one candidate therefore means decoding it into the tables
and, for each rule, entering its evidence, running the diagram and
checking whether the recommended action matches the expert's; the
fraction of matches gives the score. Degenerate tables sampled in the
early generations can occasionally make the engine fail numerically;
those rules are simply skipped rather than penalised, since the share of
unevaluable candidates vanishes quickly as the search progresses. The
$0$--$1$ indicator above is the simplest choice of per-rule loss; the
implementation also supports the smoother variants (regret, margin,
softmax, entropy) introduced in Chapter~\ref{ch:framework}, compared
experimentally in Chapter~4.

\section{The optimiser}
% ==============================================================

\textsc{EDAspy}~\cite{soloviev2024edaspy} is the reference open-source
EDA library of the UPM CIG group; it implements the univariate and
multivariate variants reviewed in Chapter~\ref{ch:framework} behind a
common interface. This work uses its continuous UMDA
(\texttt{UMDAc}) for three reasons:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the search space is high-dimensional ($d>10^{3}$ in the
        benchmark of Chapter~4), which makes the full covariance
        estimation of richer variants such as EGNA expensive;
  \item the validity constraint of Proposition~\ref{prop:valid-cpt} is
        enforced row by row, so modelling cross-parameter dependencies
        brings little benefit here;
  \item a univariate model has a single hyperparameter per dimension,
        keeping the tuning of Chapter~4 tractable.
\end{itemize}
The mechanics of UMDA — independent Gaussian marginals refitted from the
selected elite at every generation — were already given in
Chapter~\ref{ch:framework} and are not repeated here. Richer variants,
SPEDA in particular, are left to the perspectives chapter.

The run is governed by the few hyperparameters of
Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}. The search stops early when the best cost
does not improve for $T_{\text{dead}}$ consecutive generations, which
avoids wasting time once the population has converged.

\centering

\hline
\textbf{Hyperparameter} & \textbf{Symbol} & \textbf{Role}\\
\hline
\texttt{size\_gen}    & $N$               & Individuals per generation\\
\texttt{max\_iter}    & $T$               & Maximum number of generations\\
\texttt{dead\_iter}   & $T_{\text{dead}}$ & Early-stopping patience\\

The population is initialised by uniform sampling in $[0,1]^{d}$, the
\textsc{EDAspy} default. Two natural extensions, both supported by the
library and discussed in the perspectives chapter, are an LLM-generated
warm start~\cite{nafar2025llm4bn} and parallel fitness
evaluation~\cite{mendiburu2005parallel}; neither is used here, to keep
the experiments single-threaded and reproducible.

\section{Summary}
% ==============================================================

The pipeline turns the formal problem of Chapter~\ref{ch:framework} into
an executable loop: the diagram and the expert rules are loaded once, a
candidate parameter vector in $[0,1]^{d}$ is decoded into a valid model
(Proposition~\ref{prop:valid-cpt}), the diagram is evaluated against
every rule to produce the cost of Definition~\ref{def:cost}, and the
continuous UMDA of \textsc{EDAspy} searches for the vector of minimum
cost. The next chapter puts this pipeline to work on the
neonatal-jaundice benchmark of G{\'o}mez et
al.~\cite{gomez2007jaundice}.

\chapter{Theoretical Framework}
\label{ch:framework}

This chapter introduces the theoretical background that the rest of
the thesis relies on. We start with Decision Support Systems and
their graphical formulation as Influence Diagrams, fix the formal
definitions that are used in subsequent chapters and motivate the
parameter-estimation problem that we tackle. We then present
Estimation of Distribution Algorithms with the level of detail
required to justify our design choices, including the specific
variants and the convergence guarantees that ground the empirical
study of Chapter~4.

% ==============================================================
\section{Decision Support Systems and Influence Diagrams}
% ==============================================================

A \emph{Decision Support System} (DSS) is a model or piece of
software designed to assist decision-makers by facilitating access to
relevant data and by applying analytical models and techniques to
ill-structured problems~\cite{insua2005,howard2008foundations,shenoy2009eolss}.
The role of a DSS is not to replace the decision-maker but to provide
structured access to information and a principled way of evaluating
alternatives before a final decision is reached.

Among the many graphical formalisms available, \emph{Influence
Diagrams} (IDs) have established themselves as one of the most
compact and expressive
representations~\cite{howard2005influence,koller2009pgm}. An ID
is a directed acyclic graph whose nodes encode the three fundamental
ingredients of a sequential decision problem — random variables,
controllable choices and preferences — and whose arcs encode
conditional dependencies and informational precedence.

\subsection{Formal definition}

\begin{definition}[Influence Diagram]
\label{def:id}
An \emph{Influence Diagram} is a tuple
$\mathcal{D} = (\mathbf{C}, \mathbf{D}, V, \mathcal{E})$ where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{C} = \{C_1,\ldots,C_n\}$ is a finite set of
          \emph{chance nodes}, each modelling a discrete random
          variable with finite domain $\Omega_{C_i}$;
    \item $\mathbf{D} = \{D_1,\ldots,D_k\}$ is a finite set of
          \emph{decision nodes}, each with a finite action set
          $\mathcal{A}_{D_i}$;
    \item $V$ is a single \emph{utility} (value) node encoding the
          preferences of the decision-maker;
    \item $\mathcal{E}$ is a set of directed arcs split into
          probabilistic, informational and functional arcs.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

For the ID to admit a well-defined evaluation procedure the graph
must be \emph{regular}: it is a DAG, the value node has no
successors, and the decisions can be linearly ordered by a directed
path that traverses all of them. Throughout this work all IDs are
assumed to be regular.

\subsection{Quantitative parameterisation}

Each chance node $C_i$ carries a \emph{conditional probability
table} (CPT) $\theta_{C_i}$ that assigns to every configuration of
its probabilistic parents $\Pi(C_i)$ a probability distribution over
$\Omega_{C_i}$:
\[
   \theta_{C_i}\bigl(\cdot \mid \mathbf{pa}\bigr) \;\in\;
   \Delta(\Omega_{C_i})
   \qquad
   \forall \mathbf{pa}\in\prod_{C_j\in\Pi(C_i)}\Omega_{C_j},
\]
where $\Delta(\Omega_{C_i})$ denotes the probability simplex over the
states of $C_i$. The utility node carries a real-valued mapping
\[
   u : \prod_{X\in\Pi(V)}\Omega_X \longrightarrow \mathbb{R},
\]
where $\Omega_{D_i}\equiv\mathcal{A}_{D_i}$ for any decision parent.
The function $u$ encodes the preferences of the decision-maker in
the sense of Multi-Attribute Utility Theory, and when its arguments
satisfy the usual independence conditions it admits an additive or
multiplicative decomposition that drastically simplifies its
elicitation~\cite{riosinsua2002maut}. The collection of all CPTs
and the utility table is the \emph{parameter vector} of the
diagram, written $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$.

\begin{remark}[Combinatorial explosion]
\label{rem:explosion}
The number of free entries of a CPT grows multiplicatively with the
parents: a node with $m$ parents, each taking $r$ states, and $r$
own states contributes $r^{m}(r-1)$ free parameters. This
exponential growth is the source of the elicitation bottleneck
discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:introduction} and the reason why
parameter optimisation rather than exhaustive elicitation is
necessary.
\end{remark}

A \emph{decision rule} for $D_i$ is a mapping
$\delta_i : \prod_{C_j\in\Pi_{\text{info}}(D_i)}\Omega_{C_j}
\longrightarrow \mathcal{A}_{D_i}$
that prescribes an action for every observable context. The
collection $\boldsymbol{\delta}=(\delta_1,\ldots,\delta_k)$ over all
decisions is called a \emph{policy}. The expected utility of a
policy under a parameter vector $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eu}
   \mathrm{EU}(\boldsymbol{\delta}\mid\boldsymbol{\Theta}) =
   \sum_{\mathbf{c}} u\!\bigl(\mathbf{c},
                              \boldsymbol{\delta}(\mathbf{c})\bigr)
   \prod_{C_i\in\mathbf{C}}
   \theta_{C_i}\!\bigl(c_i \mid \mathbf{pa}(c_i)\bigr),
\end{equation}
and the \emph{optimal policy} is
$\boldsymbol{\delta}^{\!*}(\boldsymbol{\Theta}) =
\arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\delta}}
\mathrm{EU}(\boldsymbol{\delta}\mid\boldsymbol{\Theta})$.

\noindent The classical algorithm of
Shachter~\cite{shachter1986evaluating} computes
$\boldsymbol{\delta}^{\!*}(\boldsymbol{\Theta})$ in polynomial time
for any fixed $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ by successively reversing arcs
and eliminating nodes from the graph.

\section{The Knowledge-Acquisition Problem}
% ==============================================================

In practice the structure of the ID is elicited from the expert in a
small number of modelling sessions, but the numerical
parameterisation $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ is far harder to obtain. The
classical cycle of~\cite{bielza2000issues,bielza2010modeling} proceeds in
five stages — problem identification, enumeration of alternatives,
modelling, evaluation and sensitivity analysis — and the modelling
stage alone accounts for the bulk of the cost. As anticipated in
Remark~\ref{rem:explosion}, the size of $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ grows
exponentially with the connectivity of the diagram, so an exhaustive
elicitation is unrealistic for any diagram of practical size.

We assume instead that the expert can provide a \emph{small set of
decision rules} that need only be partial: some variables can be
declared irrelevant, in which case the rule applies for every value
of those variables.

\begin{definition}[Expert rule base]
\label{def:rules}
A \emph{rule base} is a finite set
\[
   \mathcal{R} = \bigl\{(\mathbf{x}^{(l)}, D_{i_l}, a^{(l)})\bigr\}_{l=1}^{L},
\]
where $\mathbf{x}^{(l)}$ is a (possibly partial) observation vector
on the informational parents of decision $D_{i_l}$, and
$a^{(l)}\in\mathcal{A}_{D_{i_l}}$ is the action the expert deems
optimal under $\mathbf{x}^{(l)}$. Components marked as
``irrelevant'' contribute no constraint and the rule must hold for
every value they may take.
\end{definition}

Given such a rule base we measure the consistency of a parameter
vector with the expert by a simple accuracy:

\begin{definition}[Fitness function]
\label{def:fitness}
The fitness of $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ on $\mathcal{R}$ is
\[
   f(\boldsymbol{\Theta}) =
   \frac{1}{|\mathcal{R}|} \sum_{(\mathbf{x}^{(l)},D_{i_l},a^{(l)})\in\mathcal{R}}
   \mathbb{1}\!\left[\delta^{*}_{i_l}(\boldsymbol{\Theta},
                                       \mathbf{x}^{(l)}) = a^{(l)}\right]
   \;\in\;[0,1],
\]
where $\delta^{*}_{i_l}(\boldsymbol{\Theta},\mathbf{x}^{(l)})$ is the
action prescribed by the optimal policy of the ID under
$\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ when the evidence $\mathbf{x}^{(l)}$ is
entered. Irrelevant components are averaged over.
\end{definition}

The parameter-recovery problem is then the constrained search
\[
   \boldsymbol{\Theta}^{\star} =
   \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\Theta}} f(\boldsymbol{\Theta}),
\]
with $\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ ranging over the product of all valid
CPTs and utility tables. The next proposition justifies the use of
a derivative-free metaheuristic instead of a gradient-based
optimiser.

\begin{proposition}[Non-smoothness of $f$]
\label{prop:nonsmooth}
The fitness $f$ of Definition~\ref{def:fitness} is in general
non-convex and not differentiable with respect to
$\boldsymbol{\Theta}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
The optimal policy is obtained through an $\arg\max$ over a sum of
products of the parameters. Any infinitesimal perturbation of
$\boldsymbol{\Theta}$ that flips the $\arg\max$ at some rule causes
a unit jump in the indicator of Definition~\ref{def:fitness}; hence
$f$ is piecewise constant and discontinuous. Non-convexity follows
from the non-linearity introduced by the $\arg\max$.
\end{proof}

\noindent Proposition~\ref{prop:nonsmooth} rules out gradient-based
estimators and motivates the population-based, derivative-free
strategy adopted in the rest of the document.

\section{Estimation of Distribution Algorithms}
% ==============================================================

Estimation of Distribution Algorithms
(EDAs)~\cite{larranaga2002eda,larranaga2024edas} are a class of
evolutionary metaheuristics in which the classical
crossover-and-mutation operators are replaced by the
\emph{construction and sampling of an explicit probabilistic model}
of the search space. At every generation a probability distribution
is fitted to the best individuals of the current population and used
to generate the next generation. Algorithm~\ref{alg:eda} states the
generic scheme.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Generic EDA}
\label{alg:eda}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Initialise $\mathcal{P}_0$ of $N$ individuals from a prior on
       the search space.
\State $t \leftarrow 0$
\Repeat
    \State Evaluate $f$ on every $\boldsymbol{\Theta}\in\mathcal{P}_t$.
    \State Select the top-$S$ individuals
           $\mathcal{P}_t^{\text{sel}}$.
    \State Estimate a probabilistic model $\hat{p}_t$ on
           $\mathcal{P}_t^{\text{sel}}$.
    \State Sample $N$ new individuals from $\hat{p}_t$ to form
           $\mathcal{P}_{t+1}$.
    \State $t \leftarrow t+1$
\Until{stopping criterion is met}
\State \Return best individual found.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The defining choice of an EDA is the family used for $\hat{p}_t$:
richer families capture more structure of the search space at the
expense of a higher estimation cost.

\subsection{Variants relevant for this work}

Because the parameter vector of an ID lives in a continuous space
(every CPT entry and every utility value is a real number), only
continuous EDAs are relevant. The three variants considered in this
thesis are:

\paragraph{UMDA\textsubscript{c}.} In the continuous Univariate
Marginal Distribution Algorithm~\cite{larranaga2002eda} each dimension
is modelled by an independent Gaussian:
\[
    \hat{p}_t^{j}(\phi_j) = \mathcal{N}\bigl(\phi_j;\,
    \hat{\mu}_t^{j},\,(\hat{\sigma}_t^{j})^{2}\bigr),
\]
with mean and variance estimated by the sample moments of the
selected individuals. UMDA\textsubscript{c} is the cheapest and most
scalable variant but cannot capture inter-variable dependencies.

\paragraph{EGNA.} The Estimation of Gaussian Networks
Algorithm~\cite{larranaga2002eda} fits a Gaussian Bayesian network
to $\mathcal{P}_t^{\text{sel}}$ and samples from it. It captures
pairwise (and higher-order) linear dependencies between the
parameters of the model but pays a cost of $O(d^2 N)$ per generation
for a $d$-dimensional problem with $N$ individuals.

\paragraph{KEDA and SPEDA.} Recent variants replace the Gaussian
assumption by kernel density estimators (KEDA) or mix Gaussian and
non-parametric nodes (SPEDA)~\cite{soloviev2023speda}. They are of
interest for multi-modal problems and are listed in the perspectives
chapter.

\subsection{Selection and exploration--exploitation}

The most common selection scheme — and the one adopted in this work
— is \emph{truncation}: only the top fraction $\mu$ of the population
contributes to $\hat{p}_t$.

\begin{proposition}[Diversity--convergence trade-off]
\label{prop:tradeoff}
Under truncation selection with ratio $\mu\in(0,1)$, decreasing
$\mu$ raises the selection pressure, shrinks the support of the
estimated model and accelerates convergence at the cost of an
increased risk of premature convergence to a local optimum.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
As $\mu\to 0$ the selected set shrinks to the current best
individual and $\hat{p}_t$ concentrates its mass arbitrarily close to
it. As $\mu\to 1$ no information is extracted from the fitness
function and the algorithm degenerates to uniform random search.
The optimal trade-off lies in between and is problem-dependent.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Constraint handling: simplex projection}

The CPT rows of the ID must lie on the probability simplex
$\Delta^{r-1}=\{p\in\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^{r}\mid\sum_i p_i = 1\}$.
Gaussian samples in $\mathbb{R}^d$ generally violate this constraint
and must be repaired.

\begin{definition}[Euclidean simplex projection]
The projection of $\mathbf{v}\in\mathbb{R}^{r}$ onto $\Delta^{r-1}$
is the unique vector
\[
    \Pi_{\Delta}(\mathbf{v}) = \arg\min_{\mathbf{p}\in\Delta^{r-1}}
    \lVert\mathbf{p}-\mathbf{v}\rVert_2.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Sorting algorithm of Duchi et al.]
\label{prop:duchi}
$\Pi_{\Delta}(\mathbf{v})$ can be computed exactly in $O(r\log r)$
time by sorting $\mathbf{v}$ and finding a threshold $\rho$ such that
$p_i=\max(v_i-\rho,0)$ satisfies $\sum_i p_i = 1$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
See~\cite{duchi2008projections} for the standard sorting-based
derivation.
\end{proof}

The implementation chapter exploits a simpler $\ell_1$ normalisation
that is equivalent to $\Pi_{\Delta}$ when the candidate vector is
non-negative, which can be enforced by clipping the EDA samples to
$[0,1]^d$.

\subsection{Convergence under elitism}

A final theoretical fact justifies the use of elitism — the
preservation of the best individual across generations — that we
adopt in Chapter~\ref{ch:implementation}.

\begin{theorem}[Asymptotic convergence of an elitist EDA]
\label{thm:convergence}
Let $f^{\star}=\sup_{\boldsymbol{\Theta}} f(\boldsymbol{\Theta})$ and
let $f^{\max}_t$ be the fitness of the best individual in
$\mathcal{P}_t$. If the algorithm preserves at least one copy of
the best individual at every generation (elitism) and the model
$\hat{p}_t$ assigns positive probability to every region of positive
volume of the search space, then
\[
    \lim_{t\to\infty} f^{\max}_t = f^{\star}
    \qquad \text{almost surely.}
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
Elitism makes the sequence $\{f^{\max}_t\}$ monotone
non-decreasing. Positive support of $\hat{p}_t$ ensures that every
neighbourhood of an optimum is sampled with positive probability at
every step; a Borel--Cantelli argument shows that the probability of
never sampling an $\varepsilon$-optimal point decays to zero for any
$\varepsilon>0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:convergence} is asymptotic and does not specify a
convergence rate. The rate depends on the model family, the
selection ratio and the population size, all of which must be tuned
empirically — as we do in Chapter~4.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Comparison with classical Genetic Algorithms}

It is illuminating to compare an EDA with the standard Genetic
Algorithm (GA) approach used in the predecessor of this
work~\cite{henriquez2024tfm}. A univariate EDA with Gaussian
marginals performs, in effect, an \emph{implicit uniform crossover}
over the selected population: sampling independently from each
marginal is equivalent to drawing each gene from a uniformly chosen
parent. The main advantage of an EDA over a GA is that the
probabilistic model is built and inspected \emph{explicitly}, which
makes the dependency structure of the search space visible and
exploitable. A second advantage, crucial in our setting, is that
the EDA needs no problem-specific crossover operator; designing such
an operator for vectors constrained to a product of simplices is far
from trivial.

\section{Summary}
% ==============================================================

We have introduced the formal machinery needed to state the
parameter-recovery problem: the definition of an Influence Diagram,
its quantitative parameterisation and the notion of an optimal
policy obtained by the Shachter algorithm. We have then formalised
the expert rule base, the fitness function and shown
(Proposition~\ref{prop:nonsmooth}) that the resulting optimisation
problem is non-convex and non-smooth — hence the choice of a
derivative-free method. Finally, we have presented the EDA family
with the variants used in this work, the selection and constraint
machinery, and the convergence guarantee under elitism. The next
chapter turns these theoretical pieces into an executable
implementation.
